Isocrates and constitutive Logoi

Haskins highlights how Isocrates sees logos not as a way to discipline a predetermined audience (as in forensic rhetoric) but as the creative force that calls community into being. Here, Peitho is the active energy of association, the very reason people gather and listen.

The Essential Dynamic: We must acknowledge Isocrates’ own elitism. This 'community-building' was pan-Hellenic and explicitly designed to create a Greek identity in opposition to the 'barbarian' Other. The dwelling place was bounded, and his logic of Peitho remains a 'recovery' of potential rather than a perfect ideal.

Ethos as a Dwelling Place

Leaving behind Aristotle’s conceptualization of ethos as *credibility* (a product to be optimized), we follow Hyde to see ethos as a *dwelling place* (an open site). This view of ethos shifts the goal from acquiring an audience's trust to creating an open field of relation. Peitho becomes the 'atmosphere' or 'vibe' of this home, the invisible force that welcomes participation.

Feminist Invitational Rhetoric

Foss & Griffin’s framework enacts the recovery of Peitho’s agency. By rejecting the intent to conquer or persuade, invitational rhetoric moves to a structure of *offering*. This radical return to relation mirrors the original, gendered 'instability' of Peitho, where the outcome is emergent rather than guaranteed. This is not a new concept, but a recovery of the collaborative ethos already present in the non-disciplinary Peitho.

Kairos and Epideictic

This point remains an active question. How does the 'right time' (*kairos*) change when we are not seeking the perfect moment to strike, but the perfect moment to welcome? If epideictic is about community-building (reinforcing values) rather than just display, it becomes the 'shared ritual' that sustains the 'dwelling place' of ethos. This connection is key for extending this work into civic engagement and pop culture.
